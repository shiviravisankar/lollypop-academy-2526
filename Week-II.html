Laws of UX are principles derived from human psychology and behavior that guide designers in creating intuitive, effective user experiences. These laws help us understand how users interact with interfaces and make decisions.

"Good design is based on psychology. Understanding UX laws helps create experiences that feel intuitive, effortless, and enjoyable."

Jakob's Law

Users prefer familiar design patterns.

Fitts's Law

Larger, closer targets are easier to hit.

Hick's Law

More choices = longer decision time.

Law of Proximity

Related elements should be grouped.

Miller's Law

Users can hold 7¬±2 items in memory.

Aesthetic-Usability Effect

Beautiful design appears more usable.

Pareto Principle

80% of impact comes from 20% of features.

Tesler's Law

Systems should handle complexity, not users.

Law of Modularity

Breaking content into sections improves usability.

Law of Feedback

Users need immediate response to actions.

Von Restorff Effect

Unique elements stand out more.

Zeigarnik Effect

Unfinished tasks stay in memory.

Doherty Threshold

Keep system response time under 400ms.

Law of Similarity

Similar elements feel related.

Law of Uniform Connectedness

Connected elements seem related.

Postel's Law

Be flexible in input, strict in output.

Serial Position Effect

Users remember the first and last items in a list best.

Occam's Razor

The simplest solution is usually the best.

Elevating UX Beyond Usability

Emotional design taps into human psychology to create experiences that not only work well but also feel right. Whether it's a sense of delight, trust, or motivation, emotions shape how users perceive and interact with a product.

Surprise & Delight ‚Äì Google Doodles
