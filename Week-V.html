✨ Why Do We Do Synthesis in UX?

(Transforming Raw Data into Meaningful Insights)

🎯 Overview

Synthesis is the critical bridge between raw research data and actionable design insights. It helps us make sense of user behaviors, identify patterns, and develop a deeper understanding of user needs and motivations.

"Research gives us the 'what'—synthesis helps us understand the 'why.'"

To Make Sense of the Mess

Bringing order to chaos by spotting patterns, grouping similar themes, and connecting dots.

To Discover Real Insights

Going beyond summaries to extract meaning and underlying motivations.

To Find Patterns Across Users

Identifying connections across multiple data points that reveal important user patterns.

To Build Empathy with Users

Understanding pain points, goals, mindsets, and moments of tension.

To Generate Design Opportunities

Creating foundations for ideation, prototyping, and testing.

To Align the Team

Building shared understanding and removing subjective assumptions.

"We do synthesis to transform messy research into meaningful insights and actionable design directions. It's where real user-centered design begins."

🧩 Affinity Mapping

(Finding Patterns in Chaos)

Affinity mapping is a key synthesis method where we group related data points to identify patterns or themes. It helps us organize scattered information into meaningful clusters that reveal deeper insights.

Activity 1: Affinity Mapping Exercise

Brief:

Group the following Tamil personalities into logical categories.

M.G. Ramachandran, J. Jayalalithaa, Kamal Haasan, Vijayakanth, Udhayanidhi Stalin, Dhanush, Vijay Sethupathi, Suriya, Rajinikanth, Vishal, Raghava Lawrence, Aishwarya Rajinikanth

Task:

  1. Work in teams to cluster these names into logical groups
  2. Name each cluster based on common themes
  3. Discuss why you placed certain personalities in specific groups

Time: 10 minutes

Deliverable: Categorized groups with names

Affinity Mapping Takeaways

Themes are Fluid, Not Fixed

Categories aren't absolute—they're emergent and created based on patterns.

Context Matters

The same data point can feel different depending on the lens you apply.

Practice Prioritization

Ask: "What is this data most strongly indicating right now?"

Justify Your Decisions

Explain why something belongs in a cluster to stakeholders.

There's No "Correct" Cluster

Two teams might group the same data points differently—and that's okay. What matters is whether your clustering leads to actionable understanding.

🧠 Empathy Mapping

(Understanding Users Through Four Lenses)

Empathy mapping helps us understand a user's experience through four distinct lenses: what they say, think, do, and feel. This approach creates a holistic picture of the user's world and builds deeper empathy among team members.

Activity 2: Empathy Mapping Practice

Brief:

Explore the following quote through empathy mapping:

"I usually take a cab to work because I hate the unpredictability of autos. Sometimes they refuse rides, and other times the fare is just too much. I wish I didn't have to explain my destination five times in a rush."

Task:

  1. Break down this quote into the four quadrants: Say, Think, Feel, Do
  2. Go beyond what's explicitly stated to infer thoughts and feelings
  3. Consider the context and user's potential motivations

Time: 5 minutes

Deliverable: Completed empathy map

🗣️ SAY

"I hate the unpredictability of autos."

"Sometimes they refuse rides."

"The fare is just too much."

💭 THINK

"Why can't commuting be more reliable?"

"Is it worth the stress to save a little money?"

"I hope I'm not late again."

❤️ FEEL

Frustrated, anxious, mentally drained before work even begins.

Feeling helpless or not in control of the commute.

🏃 DO

Books a cab instead of taking an auto.

Tries to plan ahead.

Explains destination multiple times.

Likely checks traffic or fare apps often.

👤 Creating Personas

(Bringing User Archetypes to Life)

Personas are composite characters that represent different user types who might use your product in similar ways. They help teams understand and empathize with the users they're designing for, making abstract data concrete and memorable.

Activity 3: From Quotes to Personas

Brief:

Review the following quotes from interviews about commuting

  • "I leave by 8 am and need something predictable. I prefer booking through an app, even if it's slightly expensive. I don't want to bargain with auto drivers. Also, I feel safer knowing the driver details are on the app."
  • "I use my scooter most of the time. But when it rains or when I need to carry stuff, I take a local auto. I prefer calling my usual auto anna directly. He knows my timings."
  • "I travel 2–3 times a week, mostly mid-day. I check both Ola and Uber, but often there's no availability or surge pricing. Sometimes I call a local cab guy I know."
  • "We usually take share-autos or buses. It's cheap and we don't mind waiting. If we're going out in a group, we pool in for a cab. I don't use apps much unless I'm traveling late."
  • "I don't use smartphones for booking. I usually call my neighbor's son to drop me or take a taxi from the stand. I don't like talking to new drivers."

Task:

  1. Identify common patterns or user types from these quotes
  2. Create a persona for one user type you identify
  3. Include their goals, frustrations, and behaviors

Time: 15 minutes

Deliverable: One complete persona

Example Personas

Ramya – The App-First Commuter

Age: 26

Occupation: Software Developer


🟢 Goals:

  • Quick, predictable, and safe daily commute
  • Minimal interaction or negotiation

🔴 Frustrations:

  • Bargaining with local drivers
  • Unreliable driver behavior

🚕 Commuting Behavior:

  • Always uses ride-hailing apps
  • Willing to pay extra for convenience and safety
  • Prioritizes digital trust (e.g., knowing driver details)

🔧 How Might We Questions:

  • How might we make digital bookings more transparent and stress-free?
  • How might we ensure a consistent and trusted driver experience for app users?
  • How might we build features that eliminate the need for fare negotiation?

Sundar – The Relationship-Based Local Rider

Age: 41

Occupation: Small Business Owner

🟢 Goals:

  • Reliable, on-time transport
  • Minimal effort in coordinating rides

🔴 Frustrations:

  • Unfamiliar drivers
  • Having to repeat instructions or locations

🚕 Commuting Behavior:

  • Uses personal contacts (known auto drivers)
  • Switches to local autos or taxis when needed
  • Values familiarity and routine

🔧 How Might We Questions:

  • How might we let users book their regular/local driver more easily?
  • How might we help drivers remember recurring users' preferences?
  • How might we create a seamless way to mix tech convenience with local trust?

🛠️ Working Session & Prep Check

(45–60 minutes)

Project Status Roundtable

Brief:

Share your current project status with your group to get feedback and identify gaps before next week's deep dive.

Task:

  1. Report on the number of interviews or data sources you have
  2. Describe the format of your notes (quotes, behaviors, etc.)
  3. Share any patterns you've already noticed

Time: 10-15 minutes

Deliverable: Verbal status update

Is Your Research Ready for Synthesis?

  • At least 2–3 user interviews or data points
  • Raw notes are written clearly
  • Patterns or repeat behaviors spotted
  • Quotes and observations separated
  • Notes organized (Affinity map-friendly)
