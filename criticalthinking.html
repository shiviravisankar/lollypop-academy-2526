Are Biases and Assumptions Always Wrong in UX?

  • We often hear that biases and assumptions are bad, but are they always?
  • Can we design a user experience without making any assumptions?

Biases are mental shortcuts that help us process information quickly. They aren't inherently bad, but they can lead to design decisions that exclude or misrepresent users. Some common UX-related biases include:

  • Confirmation Bias ‐ Favoring data that supports our existing beliefs.
  • Recency Bias ‐ Giving more weight to recent feedback rather than long-term trends.
  • Cultural Bias ‐ Designing for one's own cultural norms and neglecting diversity.

"When starting a project, you assume users want a feature before testing it. Is this assumption bad? Or does it give us a starting point for research?"

"What happens if we design without any assumptions? Wouldn't that mean endless research with no decisions made?"

Assumptions drive the initial stages of design.

Without them, we wouldn't have hypotheses to test. However, the key is to validate assumptions through research and data rather than blindly relying on them.

For Example,

Facebook's "Year in Review"

Facebook auto-generated a "Year in Review" for users, highlighting their most engaging photos. While many users were delighted, for some the algorithm resurfaced painful memories, like a loved one's passing or a tragic event. Facebook assumed that engagement = happiness, without critically evaluating how different contexts could impact the user experience.

What other metrics could have been evaluated to create this review?

LinkedIn's Biased Job Recommendation Algorithm

LinkedIn's job recommendation algorithm tended to suggest higher-paying jobs to men than to women, even when their profiles were similar. The algorithms learned from existing hiring patterns, reinforcing systemic gender bias.

UX and AI teams must audit algorithms for bias, what unseen factors are influencing decisions?
