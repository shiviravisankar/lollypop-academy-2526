üß© Activity 1: Problem Dump & Thematic Clustering

(Finding Patterns in Food Experiences)

Activity Description

Step 1: Individual Problem Writing (7‚Äì8 mins)

Write down all problems you experience in the world of food. Don't restrict yourself, this could be ordering, eating at a restaurant, sourcing ingredients, anything. Could be problems you've faced or seen someone face.

Be specific and personal (what annoyed, confused, or slowed them down).

Step 2: Group-Level Thematic Clustering (7‚Äì8 mins)

Cluster these problems under broad experience stages or themes: (for ex, Discovery/Info search, Booking & Checkout, Payment Flow, Trust/Security, Error Handling, Customer Support and others.)

Time: 15-16 minutes

Deliverable: Problem clusters

Key Takeaways

Problems are More Powerful When Specific

The more contextual your problem, the better the design response can be.

Multiple Problems in One Experience

By dumping raw problems, we begin to see how friction builds up across a journey ‚Äî not just in one screen or step.

Themes Reveal Patterns, Not Just Categories

Grouping similar problems makes it easier to see recurring pain points and broader opportunity spaces.

Solutions Should Not Come Immediately

Letting solutions emerge after grouping problems encourages problem framing before problem-solving.

Everyone's Problems Are Different

This builds empathy: you're not designing for "users" ‚Äî you're designing for different people with different contexts.

Vague Problem:

"The app is confusing."

Sharp Problem:

"I couldn't find where to check if my booking was confirmed."

üß† Activity 2: Insight Mining

(From Problems to Deeper Understanding)

Activity Description

Goal: Turn raw problems into insight statements.

Pick 1‚Äì2 clusters from the board.

Write multiple insight statements in the format:

"Users feel ___ because ___."

Try to write insights that are non-obvious or reveal a deeper frustration.

Examples:

  • "Users feel uncertain when booking trains because they don't understand what class/seat type gives them comfort for long journeys."
  • "Users worry about whether the payment went through because they receive no confirmation but the money is deducted."

Time: 20 minutes

Deliverable: Insight statements

Key Takeaways

Insights Are Explanations, Not Observations

Insights explain why a behavior or problem is happening, not just what is happening.

Great Insights Are Specific and Generative

A good insight feels like both a revelation and a doorway to design solutions.

You Surface Insights From Patterns

Repeated frustrations across different users or scenarios often signal a deeper unmet need or assumption.

Insights Are Not UI-Specific

They should be emotional, motivational, or behavioral rather than focused on interface elements.

Observation:

"People abandon booking midway"

Insight:

"People feel unsure if their payment went through, so they drop off"

üéØ Activity 3: JTBD Builder

(Jobs To Be Done Framework)

Activity Description

What is JTBD?

Jobs To Be Done is a framework that shifts focus from what users are doing to why they're doing it. Instead of asking: "What features do users want?" We ask: "What job is the user hiring this product to do in their life?"

Goal: Practice job framing from insight.

For each insight, write a Jobs To Be Done sentence:

"When I [situation], I want to [motivation] so that I can [expected outcome]."

Encourage realistic, relatable scenarios.

Example:

"When I'm booking a train at night for an emergency, I want to quickly know which seats are available so that I don't waste time and miss my window."

Time: 15 minutes

Deliverable: JTBD statements

Key Takeaways

It's Grounded in Real-Life Goals

You design for what matters to users, not just what's broken in the UI.

It Helps Uncover Unmet Needs

People don't always say what they need, but JTBD helps you find the underlying intent behind their actions.

It's More Future-Proof

Tech changes, but human jobs (like feeling secure, saving time, staying informed) stay consistent.

It Bridges User and Business Goals

When you know the job, you can build better solutions and make a stronger case for them to stakeholders.

"JTBD focuses on the progress a person is trying to make in a particular circumstance rather than on the product they might use to get there."

üí° Activity 4: Problem ‚Üí HMW Flip

(How Might We Questions)

Activity Description

Goal: Frame insights into design challenges.

Take top JTBDs or pain points and turn them into HMWs:

"How might we help users [goal] even when [constraint]?"

Encourage multiple HMWs per problem ‚Äî one practical, one aspirational, one weird/fun.

Examples:

  • "How might we help users feel confident their payment went through even when the app crashes?"
  • "How might we make booking a train feel like planning a vacation?"

Time: 15 minutes

Deliverable: HMW questions

Types of HMW Questions

Practical HMWs

Focus on feasible solutions to immediate problems.

Aspirational HMWs

Explore ideal scenarios without immediate constraints.

Creative HMWs

Generate unexpected or novel approaches to problems.

Practical HMW

"How might we help users confirm their order status even when notifications fail?"

Aspirational HMW

"How might we make grocery shopping feel like a personalized cooking show experience?"

Creative HMW

"How might we turn food delivery tracking into a game that reduces anxiety?"

Constraint-Focused HMW

"How might we help users discover new recipes even when they have limited ingredients?"
