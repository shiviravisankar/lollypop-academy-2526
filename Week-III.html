Why Research is the Foundation of Great Design

UX research is the process of understanding user behaviors, needs, and motivations through observation and feedback. It helps designers make informed decisions rather than relying on assumptions, leading to better usability and business outcomes.

Good UX research ensures that:

  • Products solve real user problems.
  • Design decisions are backed by data.
  • Users have a seamless and enjoyable experience.
  • Businesses reduce costly redesigns and failures.

Types of UX Research

Attitudinal Research

Focuses on what users say they do (e.g., surveys, interviews).

Behavioral Research

Observes what users actually do (e.g., usability testing, heatmaps).

Qualitative Research

Explores why users behave a certain way (e.g., interviews, ethnographic studies).

Quantitative Research

Measures what is happening using numerical data (e.g., A/B testing, analytics).

Deep Dive: Attitudinal vs. Behavioral Research

This distinction helps you understand what people say vs. what they do.

Use Attitudinal Research When:

  • ‚úÖ You need to understand user opinions, perceptions, and preferences.
  • ‚úÖ You want to learn why users make decisions.
  • ‚úÖ You're in the early discovery phase and want to explore pain points.

üí° Example Methods:

  • Surveys ‚Äì To gather opinions on feature preferences.
  • Interviews ‚Äì To explore motivations behind behaviors.
  • Focus Groups ‚Äì To discuss expectations or experiences.

üõë Limitations:

  • Users often don't accurately predict their own behavior.
  • Responses can be biased or influenced by the setting or the way questions are framed.

Use Behavioral Research When:

  • ‚úÖ You need to understand how users actually interact with a product.
  • ‚úÖ You're testing usability, friction points, or real-world usage patterns.
  • ‚úÖ You're refining an existing product or validating a prototype.

üí° Example Methods:

  • Usability Testing ‚Äì To observe where users struggle in a design.
  • Heatmaps & Session Recordings ‚Äì To track how users navigate an interface.
  • A/B Testing ‚Äì To compare different design variations based on actual user behavior.

üõë Limitations:

  • Doesn't always explain why users behave a certain way.
  • Requires more time and resources to conduct effectively.

Examples of Where Research Has Done Wonders

‚úÖ Good Research ‚Üí Product Success

  • Google Search: Continuous A/B testing and user feedback led to minimalistic, efficient search results.
  • Airbnb: Found that better photos increased bookings, leading to a company-wide photography initiative.

‚ùå Bad Research ‚Üí Product Failure

  • Google Glass: Ignored social and privacy concerns, leading to user rejection.
  • New Coke (1985): Focused only on taste tests (quantitative) but ignored emotional attachment to the original formula.

Key Takeaway

Great design starts with great research. Understanding users deeply ensures that products are intuitive, effective, and successful in the market.

Conclusion

Choosing the right research method depends on whether you need subjective opinions (attitudinal) or objective behavior data (behavioral), and whether you're looking for in-depth insights (qualitative) or measurable trends (quantitative). The best approach often combines multiple research types to balance what users say with what they actually do.

Setting Up a Structured User Interview Questionnaire for UX Research

A well-structured interview helps uncover real pain points, behaviors, and expectations without leading users toward pre-set answers.

üü¢ Good vs. Bad Interview Practices

üö´ Bad Practices:

‚ùå Asking leading or biased questions

  • "Don't you think the IRCTC app is slow and frustrating?"
  • "Wouldn't you prefer if BHIM looked like Google Pay?"
  • "Do you like this feature?" (Binary responses = limited insights)

‚ùå Asking vague or hypothetical questions

  • "How often do you book train tickets?" (What does 'often' mean?)
  • "Would you use the app if it had feature X?" (People say yes, but real behavior may differ)

‚ùå Stacking multiple questions into one

  • "Do you think the IRCTC app is slow, confusing, and hard to use?" (Which issue are they referring to?)

‚úÖ Good Practices:

‚úîÔ∏è Use open-ended questions to get detailed insights

  • "Tell me about the last time you booked a train ticket‚Äîwhat was your experience like?"
  • "Walk me through how you usually send money using UPI. What challenges do you face?"

‚úîÔ∏è Keep questions neutral and non-leading

  • "What do you think about the IRCTC app's booking process?"
  • "How do you feel about using BHIM UPI compared to other payment apps?"

‚úîÔ∏è Ask for specific examples & behaviors

  • "Can you describe a time when you faced a problem while booking a ticket?"
  • "What do you do when a UPI transaction fails? What's your next step?"

‚úîÔ∏è Follow up with why and how

  • "You mentioned the IRCTC app takes time‚Äîwhy do you think that happens?"
  • "What made you choose BHIM UPI over other apps, if at all?"

Sample Structured Interview Questionnaire

üîµ Section 1: User Background

  1. Can you tell me a little about yourself and your daily digital habits?
  2. How often do you travel by train / make UPI transactions?

üü† Section 2: Understanding the Current Experience

For IRCTC:

  1. Walk me through the last time you booked a train ticket. What went well? What was frustrating?
  2. What steps do you usually take when booking a ticket?
  3. Do you ever face issues while making payments on IRCTC? How do you handle them?
  4. How do you check train schedules and availability? Is the process easy for you?

For BHIM UPI:

  1. Can you describe the last time you made a UPI payment?
  2. What app do you usually use for UPI payments? Why?
  3. How was your first experience setting up and using BHIM UPI? Was anything confusing?
  4. What do you do when a UPI payment fails?

üü¢ Section 3: Pain Points & Opportunities

  1. What are the biggest challenges you face while using this app?
  2. Have you ever switched to another platform because of a bad experience? If yes, why?
  3. If you could change one thing about this app, what would it be and why?

üü£ Section 4: Closing Questions

  1. Is there anything else about your experience that we haven't covered but you'd like to share?
  2. Would you be open to testing a new version of the app when we redesign it?

Final Thoughts

  • Keep it conversational ‚Äì don't just go down a list; adapt based on their responses.
  • Listen more, talk less ‚Äì let users tell their stories.
  • Observe behaviors, not just words ‚Äì what they do often matters more than what they say.

In-Class Activity: "Keep the Conversation Going!"

Objective:

Help participants practice open-ended questioning and active listening by keeping a conversation flowing‚Äîwithout letting it end abruptly!

How It Works:

  • Pair Up ‚Äì Each participant pairs with a partner.
  • Pick a General Topic ‚Äì Participants choose everyday topics like:
    • Ordering food online üçî
    • Buying something from an e-commerce website üõí
    • Using a ride-hailing app üöñ
    • Planning a weekend trip üèñ
    • Booking a doctor's appointment online üè•
  • Start the Conversation ‚Äì One person starts by asking a broad, open-ended question.
  • Answer with a Question! ‚Äì The second person responds, but instead of answering directly, they must continue the conversation by framing their response as another question.
  • Keep It Going ‚Äì The goal is to continue the conversation for at least 5 rounds without letting it stall!

Reflection & Debrief:

  • What was challenging about keeping the conversation going?
  • Did any responses spark unexpected insights?
  • How did reframing answers as questions change the way you explored the topic?

Time: 15 minutes

Deliverable: Improved questioning skills
